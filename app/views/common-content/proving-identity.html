Users need to choose the application route that best suits their needs. These are:

  • Proving your identity online with GOV.UK Verify
  • Scanning and uploading identity documents to be checked by the local authority
  • Offline applications, such as in person or by post

To support their decision making, users need to be aware of the relative beneifts of each approach.

Those who are unlikely to be successful with GOV.UK Verify will be directed to aleternate routes.

GOV.UK Verify identification route

The government standard for identity verification is GOV.UK Verify. Using GOV.UK Verify, the user can securely prove their identity. The service can also recieve the user's address {{"and date of birth" if age}} from GOV.UK Verify.

Councils should also offer alternative routes to access the service if a user chooses not to use GOV.UK Verify. This should include an online and offline alternatives. See below for more detail on alternatives.

"Unlikely to verify" and "verification failed" pages

Some users may not have the documents or credit records that GOV.UK Verify needs to prove their identity.

  • GOV.UK Verify asks users questions to work out if they are likely to be verified. If it looks like they won't be verified, they are shown the "unlikely to verify" page.
  • A very small proportion of users will go through past these questions, and GOV.UK Verify will still not be able to verify their identity. These users will be told by their chosen Identity Provider that they couldn't be verified, and then be taken back to GOV.UK, where they will see a similar " verification failed" page. (Explore the example GOV.UK Verify journey as a user who cannot be verified.)

Both these pages should link to the alternative ways to access the service described below.

Alternative ways to access the service need to be shown to the user on the "proving identity" page, the "unlikely to verify" page, and the " verification failed" page.
